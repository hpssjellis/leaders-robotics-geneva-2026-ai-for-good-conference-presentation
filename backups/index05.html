2

A Proven Course Looking for a Global Stage

Nearly a decade of classroom testing. Two hardware generations. Two arXiv papers.



This is not a prototype. This course has been running in a real BC high school for nearly a decade, across two full hardware generations. The tools are documented in two arXiv preprints. The curriculum is on GitHub — MIT-licensed, free, no subscriptions. In my 2026 class, students solved 45 of 53 assignments on their own. That is the target for pilot schools.









3

Most Students Use AI. These Students Build It.

From consumer to creator — that is the shift this course makes.



Consumers vs. builders. Most students only ever use AI — they prompt it, pay for it, and trust whatever it outputs. Maker100 students collect their own data, train their own models in a browser, and deploy them onto real hardware. They use industry tools like Edge Impulse and SenseCraft — and they understand the full open pipeline underneath. No black boxes.

Demo 1: Local AI — No Cloud. No WiFi. Total Privacy.

A Gemma 4 LLM running entirely in your browser. Right now. Offline.



Watch: network unplugged. Intelligence stays on the machine. This is the local Gemma 4 PWA — running entirely in this browser, completely offline. No internet. No subscription. It installs once and works forever, anywhere. Why send your students' data to cloud monopolies when the browser in front of them can do this locally? Available free today: webmcu-ai.github.io/local-gemma4-pwa









5

Why This Matters: Sovereign AI for Every School

Cloud = a subscription tax. Local = infrastructure you own.



One curriculum. Works everywhere. Students with good internet use ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude — whatever they prefer. Students with no reliable internet install the local PWA once and are never blocked. The course only needs internet for initial setup and cloud ML training runs. A shared school connection is enough. One curriculum. Six countries. No connectivity prerequisite.









6

The Analog Firewall

Before touching a keyboard — write the code and draw the circuit by hand.



Understand it first. Then build it. Every student writes pseudocode by hand and draws a full circuit diagram before touching hardware. A peer Lead or I check it. Pass the firewall — power goes on. This one gate cuts equipment damage dramatically and forces real understanding before action.









7

Lead Three: Cooperation Built Into the Grade

The first student to solve a hard problem teaches three others. The ceiling rises together.



No competition. Cooperation is structurally rewarded. Every student owns their own board and codebase — no hiding in group work. The first to crack a hard problem teaches three others. Those three teach more. There is no single top student. The class's collective grade ceiling rises when everyone shares. Hoarding hurts you.









8

Three Kinds of Leaders

Users. Makers. Engineers. All three are essential.



Every student finds their lane. Users understand AI deeply enough to lead without building it themselves. Makers thrive when good examples exist — they build, improve, and share solutions. Engineers love the hardest problems and create tools others cannot. No one is locked into one category. All three are how the world changes.









9

AI is Your 24/7 Teaching Assistant

Use any LLM to debug, research, and understand — exactly like a real engineer.



LLMs are not cheating. They are the tool. Students use any LLM they like — local or cloud — to debug code and understand concepts, exactly as working engineers do. One real constraint: LLMs struggle with brand-new hardware. The course GitHub README is the ground truth for that. The skill we are building is not finding answers. It is knowing when an answer is wrong.









10

What a Real Class Looks Like

2026: 45 of 53 assignments solved independently. Asynchronous. Individual. Loud.



Messy, loud, and productive — by design. Students work asynchronously. Different students hit different milestones at different times — which means you never need thirty of any expensive sensor simultaneously. The completion chart is on the wall. Everyone can see who has earned a Lead marker. The local teacher runs the room. My role is to unblock specific problems at the right moments — not be a remote co-teacher.









11

Demo 2: Browser to Device — The webMCU-AI Pipeline

Train a vision model in the browser. Compile it. Push it to the chip. Done.



The whole pipeline in under two minutes. Live. No installation. No TensorFlow. No cloud. We collect images, train a neural network, compile it, and push it over WebSerial to this device — all from the browser. The firmware is raw C++ every student can read and modify. Every step is transparent. They own the entire stack.









12

The Hardware Kit

~$300 per student. Fits in a lunchbox. The instructor gets zero margin.



Everything a student needs to build real AI. In a lunchbox. The core is the XIAO ESP32-S3 Sense — fingertip-sized, with camera, microphone, WiFi, and enough compute for on-device ML. Full kit fits in the space of a hardcover novel. One labelled bin per student. One shared tool bin. That is the entire storage system. Budget 15 to 30 percent board replacement per year. Breaking things is part of the learning.









13

Industry Tools + Open Pipeline: Both, Always

Edge Impulse and SenseCraft for the resume. webMCU-AI for the understanding.



One without the other is incomplete. Cloud tools are what employers recognise. Students learn them. The open pipeline is what builds real understanding. Students learn that too. A graduate who only knows cloud tools is dependent on a vendor. A graduate who knows both can work anywhere — with or without internet, with or without a budget.









14

Two Cohorts. One Question. One Year.

How much expert support does a new school actually need?



The pilot is designed to answer the scaling question. Three Passionate Generalist schools — educators who commit to doing every assignment with their students. They get three zoom check-ins with me across the year. Three Technology Specialist schools — experienced educators who run independently from the GitHub alone. If generalist schools succeed with three calls, this model scales globally with minimal instructor time. Either outcome gives us real data.
